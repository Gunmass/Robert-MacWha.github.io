What Are Autoencoders

An autoencoder is a unsupervised hourglass-shaped neural networks designed to compress images into a smaller 'latent dimension' and then reconstruct the original image. While this task would normally be very difficult, by only training the network on a single category of images the autoencoder is able to extract the key features of the images.

Autoencoders can be used in a wide variety of applications such as image generation, noise reduction, feature extraction and recommendation systems. Despite this versatility they are being replaced by more powerful architectures such as GANs, CNNs, and LSTMs.

Model Summary

Photo by Matthew Stewart

Autoencoders are made up of two models, the encoder and a decoder. The encoder takes in a full size image and compresses it down into a smaller 'latent dimension', usually only around a hundred numbers long. The decoder attempts to convert the latent representation of the image back into the original image.

There are, in general, two kinds of autoencoders. DCAEs use convolutional layers to process higher-resolution images with fewer parameters while traditional autoencoders use a small number of dense layers to efficiently reduce the dimensionality of smaller images.

The Encoder

The encoder's job is to take in a full sized image and compress it down into the 'latent dimension.' To do this it will, in general, use a collection of convolutional and pooling layers to slowly decrease the size of the image until a desired dimensionality is reached. Often the layer will then be flattened and sent though a dense layer so that correlations spanning the entire image can be found.

The encoder is often much simpler than the decoder as it has much less work to do. Using my model as an example, the decoder has ~11.3 million parameters while the encoder has just 2.9 million.

The Decoder

The decoder model takes in a latent representation of an image and attempts to reconstruct the original image. This is a much more difficult task than simply encoding an image so decoders often have many times the number of parameters as the encoder.
